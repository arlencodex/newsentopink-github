ISRO's Next Moon Mission Cleared for 2027 Launch Window

India's space agency confirms Chandrayaan-4 is firmly on track, armed with a new generation of instruments designed to hunt for subsurface water ice — and potentially change the future of lunar habitation.

India's space programme cleared a critical milestone on Monday as the Indian Space Research Organisation formally announced the Chandrayaan-4 mission has received full budgetary and technical approval, with the launch window locked between March and June 2027 from the Satish Dhawan Space Centre in Sriharikota.

Key Facts at a Glance

  • Chandrayaan-4 targets a polar orbit to map the lunar south pole's permanently shadowed craters.
  • A new Ground-Penetrating Radar (GPR) will scan up to 5 metres below the lunar surface.
  • Mission cost is estimated at ₹2,104 crore, a 38% increase over Chandrayaan-3.
  • Total mission duration: 14 months, with data relay via ISRO's Deep Space Network in Bylalu.
  • First time India will attempt a dual-lander configuration on a single mission.

What Makes Chandrayaan-4 Different

Unlike its predecessors, Chandrayaan-4 will not simply confirm the presence of water molecules on the lunar surface — a feat already accomplished by Chandrayaan-1's Moon Impact Probe in 2008 and bolstered by Chandrayaan-3's Pragyan rover in 2023. This time, the mission is designed to answer a far more operationally important question: how much water ice exists in usable concentrations, and at what depth does it begin?

"This is not a discovery mission. This is a resource-mapping mission. We are preparing India to be a stakeholder in the lunar economy."

— Dr. S. Somanath, Chairman, ISRO, speaking at the mission briefing in Bengaluru

At the heart of the payload is a Ground-Penetrating Radar developed jointly by the Space Applications Centre in Ahmedabad and the Physical Research Laboratory, capable of imaging soil stratification to a depth of five metres — a world first for a lunar surface mission. The instrument will complement a Laser-Induced Breakdown Spectroscopy (LIBS) module, which can identify element concentrations in regolith samples without physical contact.

The Dual-Lander Strategy

One of the most technically ambitious elements of the mission is the dual-lander configuration. Two independent lander modules — codenamed Shakti and Pragati — will separate after entering lunar orbit and target landing zones approximately 180 km apart, both within the permanently shadowed regions near the Shackleton Crater at the south pole.

Mission scientists explain that operating two landers simultaneously allows cross-validation of subsurface data and significantly reduces the risk of a single-point failure invalidating the entire scientific return — a hard lesson internalised after the Chandrayaan-2 Vikram lander's crash-landing in September 2019.

International Collaboration

ISRO has confirmed a formal scientific partnership with the European Space Agency, which will contribute a miniaturised mass spectrometer to one of the landers. Japan's JAXA will provide advanced thermal imaging sensors, extending observation capability into the deep-cold regions where temperatures can drop to −230°C. NASA has offered Deep Space Network bandwidth support but is not contributing hardware, following a mutual agreement reached at the Artemis Accords working group in February 2026.

The Larger Stakes

The geopolitical context of the mission is impossible to ignore. China's Chang'e-7 mission, currently scheduled for late 2026, has the same target zone and virtually the same scientific objective. India and China are, for the first time, in a direct, parallel race to characterise the most strategically valuable real estate in the solar system — the water-ice deposits that could, if commercially extractable, serve as fuel depots for deep-space missions without the enormous cost of launching water from Earth.

Indian officials were careful to frame Chandrayaan-4 as a scientific endeavour, but the Ministry of Science and Technology's accompanying statement made the national interest clear: India intends to establish a permanent robotic research station at the lunar south pole by 2035, and Chandrayaan-4's data will form the geological foundation of that plan.

For now, the next critical milestone is the completion of lander integration, scheduled for December 2026, followed by a full-stack wet rehearsal at the launch complex in January 2027. If those milestones are met, India may become the second country — after the United States — to operate a multi-lander mission on the Moon's surface simultaneously.
